Detailed Symptoms of G6PD Deficiency

Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency, a genetic disorder affecting the enzyme that is crucial for protecting red blood cells from oxidative damage, manifests in a variety of symptoms that can range from mild to severe, depending on individual genetic variations and environmental triggers. The most prominent symptom of G6PD deficiency is hemolytic anemia, which is a condition characterized by the premature destruction of red blood cells. This destruction leads to a decrease in red cell mass and can trigger episodes of acute anemia, particularly after exposure to certain stressors that increase oxidative stress in the blood.

Individuals with G6PD deficiency often do not exhibit symptoms until they are exposed to certain medications, foods, or even infections that increase oxidative stress. Common triggers include certain antibiotics like sulfonamides, antimalarial drugs such as primaquine, and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). Additionally, consuming fava beans, which contain high levels of vicine and convicine, can lead to a severe reaction known as favism. This condition triggers acute hemolytic anemia in susceptible individuals, characterized by symptoms such as sudden weakness, dizziness, rapid heartbeat, and jaundice.

Jaundice, another significant indicator of G6PD deficiency, particularly in newborns, results from an accumulation of bilirubin in the blood. This yellow pigment is a byproduct of the breakdown of red blood cells. In normal situations, the liver processes bilirubin, but in individuals with G6PD deficiency, an excessive breakdown of red blood cells can overload this process, leading to visible yellowing of the skin and eyes. If not promptly and effectively treated, severe jaundice can lead to kernicterus, a potentially life-threatening form of brain damage.

Chronic symptoms of G6PD deficiency may also include ongoing mild to moderate anemia, which can contribute to fatigue and reduced physical endurance. During hemolytic episodes, individuals may experience back pain, abdominal pain, and an enlarged spleen. The severity of symptoms can vary greatly between individuals, and some may remain asymptomatic until a triggering oxidative event occurs. Due to its genetic nature, G6PD deficiency is often diagnosed in early childhood, but some cases may remain undetected until later in life, especially in regions where routine newborn screening does not include tests for this enzyme deficiency. Awareness and education about the disorder and its triggers are key to managing symptoms and preventing complications associated with this condition.
